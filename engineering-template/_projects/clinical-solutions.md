## Clinical Problem–Driven Solutions

Many clinical problems exist in the space between research insight and deployable care. The projects below focused on structured clinical problem definition, safety-constrained design, and iterative solution development under realistic medical, ethical, and regulatory considerations. Rather than optimizing for novelty, these efforts emphasized patient safety, clinician usability, and accountability over time.

# Post-Operative Forefoot Off-Loading for Hallux Rigidus Fusion

## Clinical Problem

Following hallux rigidus fusion surgery, patients are commonly placed into a partial weight-bearing period lasting several weeks. Existing post-operative footwear options, such as reverse camber shoes and CAM boots, reduce forefoot loading but often compromise balance, comfort, and natural gait mechanics. These limitations increase fall risk, reduce mobility, and rely heavily on patient compliance, particularly in older populations.

## Design Objective

The objective was to preserve patient mobility and balance during the forefoot off-loading period while minimizing pressure at the surgical site. The solution needed to remain compatible with existing post-operative protocols, reduce reliance on patient behavioral compliance, and avoid introducing new instability or safety risks.

## Approach

The design process emphasized clinical constraints first, followed by functional requirements related to stability, comfort, and load redistribution. Existing solutions were evaluated through literature review, stakeholder analysis, and a weighted decision matrix. The final concept integrated a stable full-length outsole with a highly compressible forefoot region, allowing patients to maintain a more natural walking pattern while mechanically preventing force transmission to the surgical site.

## Outcome & Insight

The resulting concept, **FlexiFoot**, combines the stability of flat-soled post-operative shoes with the off-loading benefits of reverse camber designs. By decoupling horizontal stability from vertical load transmission, the design reduces forefoot pressure without forcing exaggerated gait compensation. Importantly, the project highlighted how small mechanical design choices—such as independent outsole segmentation—can significantly affect safety, comfort, and patient adherence.

## Why This Matters for Medicine

This project reinforced the importance of designing clinical solutions that account for real patient behavior rather than assuming ideal compliance. Effective post-operative care depends not only on biomechanical correctness but also on usability, comfort, and safety in everyday contexts—factors that directly influence recovery outcomes.

# Non-Invasive Pressure Modulation for Sunken Skin Flap Syndrome

## Clinical Problem

Sunken Skin Flap Syndrome (SSFS) is a complication following decompressive craniectomy in which atmospheric pressure exceeds intracranial pressure, leading to neurological deterioration, pain, and impaired mobility. Existing interventions—such as Trendelenburg positioning or vacuum plaster casts—are temporary, uncomfortable, and carry additional risks including thrombosis, infection, and limited patient mobility.

## Design Objective

The goal was to develop a non-invasive, adjustable system capable of safely modulating pressure over the cranial defect while allowing patient mobility and minimizing clinician workload. The solution needed to prioritize safety, precise control, and long-term usability without introducing implant-associated risks.

## Approach

Multiple concepts were evaluated, including implantable scaffolds and shape-memory biomaterials. These options were ultimately rejected due to infection risk and invasiveness. The selected design employed an external pressure and depth-modulating vacuum chamber with real-time sensor feedback. This approach allowed gradual, controlled pressure adjustment while maintaining portability and minimizing clinician intervention.

## Outcome & Insight

The final concept demonstrated how closed-loop feedback and conservative pressure modulation can reduce neurological risk while improving patient comfort. Beyond the device itself, the project emphasized the importance of aligning engineering solutions with clinical workflows, regulatory pathways, and ethical constraints—particularly when failure modes carry significant neurological consequences.

## Why This Matters for Medicine

SSFS illustrates how physiological pressure imbalances can produce severe functional outcomes. This project strengthened my understanding of how clinical decision-making must balance therapeutic benefit, safety margins, patient experience, and regulatory feasibility—principles central to both translational research and clinical practice.
